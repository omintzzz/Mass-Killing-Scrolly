Analysis of Race and Mass Shootings in the US

An analysis of how the racial identities of victims and perpetrators are affected by mass killings.

The Mass Killings database, from the Associated Press, USA Today, and Northeastern University features data of killings from 2006-2025. A mass killing is described as involving four or more people killed in an incident.

Geographic Overview of the Mass Killings

This map shows the locations of mass killings across the US. Each circle represents an incident, with larger circles indicating higher casualties.

The data allows us to understand patterns of the locations for each mass killing incident along with the number of victims and their races.

Out of the mass killings included in this database, 12 were motivated by hate . From these hate-based killings, half targeted specific races. The most lethal attack was located in El Paso, Texas, where the majority of the victims were identified as Hispanic/Latino.

The largest mass killing recorded was in Las Vegas, Nevada, in 2017, where 60 people were killed and 867 were injured.

Overall Killings by Race

For all races, there were more victims than offenders. According to Professor James Fox, a key contributor to this database, “The overall majority of mass killings were not interracial or race motivated and the press, as you know, don't print race so it's hard to find.”

When discussing how the race of victims and offenders was gathered, it was not as simple as looking at these individuals. “When I first started on this project, we started collecting race based on names and photographs and then the AP said you can't do that AP rules say you can't get race based on photographs and names can be misleading,” said Fox.

Offender Outcome by Race

Analyzing the outcomes of mass killing offenders based on race.

In most mass killings, offenders have different outcomes based on race. The data shows disparities in arrests, suicides, and police confrontations.

Conclusion

From examining the dataset, and discussing further with Professor Fox he determined that "One contrary to the public perception is that this [mass killings] is not an epidemic and, in fact, not even increasing over the past 20 years" This is because population is about 50% larger than it was in the earlier 80s, when he began researching mass killings. "But back in 2006, when the data began, there really hasn’t been much of a change. There has been about an 11% increase in population."

Fox also believes that from his research, mass killings are rarely indiscriminate; in most cases, offenders specifically target people they know for personal reasons. Most of the cases in this dataset do not involve race or are hate-based as about 45% of cases are within families and 25% are gang or drug-related. “It is important that when we report the data, we distinguish the kind of cases that worry people”.
